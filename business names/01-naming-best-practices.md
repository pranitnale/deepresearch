# Naming Best Practices (research findings)

Evidence-based principles for a memorable B2B company name, researched July 2026.

## 1. Maximize processing fluency: pick a name that is effortless to say and read

Names the brain processes easily are judged more familiar, safer, and more trustworthy. A Princeton study (Alter & Oppenheimer) found newly listed stocks with fluent, easy-to-pronounce names outperformed hard-to-pronounce ones short-term (a $1,000 investment yielded ~$112 more after one trading day), and later work links name fluency to broader investor recognition and higher firm value. For a B2B parent company, this is the single strongest evidence-based lever.

Source: https://www.princeton.edu/news/2006/05/29/study-stock-performance-tied-ease-pronouncing-companys-name

## 2. Front-load a plosive consonant (b, d, g, k, p, t)

Hard 'stop' consonants, especially at the start of a word, measurably improve brand recognition, recall and memorability. A classic content analysis of top brands found they disproportionately began with letters like b, c, k and p (four plosives). Plosives create a sharp acoustic edge that aids encoding and retrieval.

Source: https://daily.jstor.org/whats-brand-name-sounds-persuasion/

## 3. Use vowel sound symbolism deliberately (front vs. back vowels)

Vowels carry sub-conscious size/energy cues. High front vowels (the 'ee/i' in 'flea', 'mini') connote small, fast, light, agile; low back vowels (the 'oh/ah/oo' in 'boot', 'bold') connote large, powerful, solid. Choose the vowel palette that matches the desired positioning (e.g. back vowels to signal a substantial, trustworthy holding company).

Source: https://www.theidbureau.com/blog/using-sound-symbolism

## 4. Keep it short: 1-3 syllables and roughly 4-8 characters

Short names cycle more easily through the phonological loop of short-term memory, so they stick better and resist being abbreviated (names over ~3 syllables get truncated, e.g. Philadelphia to 'Philly'). Branding guides converge on one-to-three syllables as the sweet spot; most highly recalled tech names (Google, Stripe, Slack) are one or two.

Source: https://fabrikbrands.com/branding-matters/brand-naming/how-many-syllables-should-a-business-name-have/

## 5. Pass the 'say it, spell it' test — avoid creative misspellings

If people cannot spell the name correctly after hearing it once, they cannot find it, type the domain, or reproduce it. Experimental work found consumers were up to 34% more likely to buy from a correctly spelled brand ('Lift') than a stylized misspelling ('Lyft'); odd spellings reduce linguistic fluency and can read as inauthentic unless the twist adds genuine meaning. Especially relevant for a Sanskrit-rooted name aimed at German speakers.

Source: https://theconversation.com/misspelled-names-may-give-brands-a-lyft-if-the-spelling-isnt-too-weird-256792

## 6. Favor distinctive (coined/arbitrary) over descriptive names — for memory and legal strength

On the trademark distinctiveness spectrum, fanciful/coined marks (e.g. Kodak, Exxon) and arbitrary marks (Apple for computers) get the broadest legal protection, while merely descriptive names are weak or unregistrable. Distinctive names are also more ownable and memorable. A Sanskrit root used arbitrarily in Europe naturally lands in the strong 'arbitrary/coined' zone.

Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trademark_distinctiveness

## 7. Treat tech suffixes (-ai, -ly, -io, -ify) as optional seasoning, not the core

Suffixes like -ify signal transformation and -ai signals AI focus, but naming experts warn they are a short-term strategy that dates quickly (compared to bolting '.com' onto 1990s names) and can trip trademark/genericness issues. For a parent/holding company meant to outlast any single AI product, a suffix that hard-codes 'AI' can become limiting; if used, keep it detachable from the root.

Source: https://news.crunchbase.com/venture/startup-names-trends-cool/

## 8. Leave room to grow — do not lock the name to one product or sector

Holding/parent-company naming guidance stresses breadth: the name should stay relevant as the company expands across industries and shifts focus, so avoid references tied to a single product, technology, or vertical. Alphabet is the canonical example of a parent name chosen for range. Given the company spans multiple sectors before focusing on energy, an abstract, evocative root beats a literal 'AI-energy' construction.

Source: https://wyomingllcattorney.com/Form-a-Wyoming-LLC/Holding-Company-Setup/Naming-Ideas

## 9. Signal trust and seriousness, and vet the name internationally and legally early

For B2B, the name must convey reliability and stability to corporate buyers and partners, work in legal/contractual contexts, and be culturally neutral across markets. Run trademark and cross-language/cultural checks at the start of the process, not the end, to avoid embarrassing meanings or costly conflicts. A Sanskrit word should be screened for unintended meanings in German, English and other target languages.

Source: https://avreafoster.com/blog/b2b-marketer-brand-name-considerations/

## 10. Aim for an evocative name over a purely descriptive or functional one

Igor's naming taxonomy ranks names from functional/descriptive (weakest positioning power) up through invented and experiential to evocative (strongest). Evocative names carry positioning, associations and story rather than literally describing the product, making them more powerful and more ownable — though harder to clear legally. A meaningful Sanskrit root (e.g. connoting light, knowledge, energy) can give evocative depth while reading as a clean coined word to Europeans.

Source: https://www.igorinternational.com/process/naming-tools-name-creation.php

## 11. Align the name's meaning with its sound (sound-meaning congruence) to deepen memory

Experimental research on associative memory shows that when a coined word's sound symbolism is congruent with its intended meaning ('big things sound big'), people remember the name-meaning pairing significantly better than incongruent pairs. So a Sanskrit root whose meaning matches its acoustic feel (e.g. a solid, back-vowel sound for a substantial energy/AI mission) will be more memorable than an arbitrary pairing.

Source: https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s41235-016-0047-y
